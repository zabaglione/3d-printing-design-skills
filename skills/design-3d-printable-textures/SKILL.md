# Design 3D-Printable Textures

Turn a visual, tactile, or structural pattern into explicit manufacturing geometry or a controlled slicer operation. Treat rendering appearance and printable shape as different deliverables.

## Load the relevant references

- Read [references/texture-taxonomy.md](references/texture-taxonomy.md) to choose a geometric representation and generator.
- Read [references/mapping-and-printability.md](references/mapping-and-printability.md) before wrapping, baking, exporting, or approving a texture.
- Read [references/source-notes.md](references/source-notes.md) only when tracing the research basis or selecting an implementation tool.
- Use `$design-functional-3d-prints` to evaluate build orientation, overhangs, bridges, islands, splitting, slicer support, custom CAD support, and process changes. Also use it when the texture changes a load path, minimum section, flexibility, ventilation, sealing, or production process.
- Use `$design-3d-printed-joints` when the texture crosses a fit, sliding surface, snap, hinge, fastener, or assembly datum.

## Hard boundary

Classify the requested result before modeling:

- **Physical structure:** changes topology, silhouette, porosity, stiffness, or motion. Model curves, strands, cells, beams, shells, or solids.
- **Surface relief:** changes the physical surface without replacing the base form. Create embossed, debossed, or symmetric displacement geometry.
- **Manufacturing texture:** generated by a slicer or process setting. Record the machine-specific operation and do not claim it exists in the source mesh.
- **Appearance only:** color, roughness, normal, bump, or shader data with no guaranteed surface displacement. Keep it as rendering or material intent, or convert and bake it before calling it printable geometry.

Reject a geometry workflow when the user only needs a render. Reject an appearance-only workflow when touch, grip, shadow, fluid behavior, silhouette, or printed relief matters.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the texture contract

Record:

- purpose: identity, touch, grip, wear masking, light diffusion, airflow, flexibility, stiffness, or decoration;
- target regions and protected regions;
- viewing and touch distance;
- part scale, surface curvature, and intended orientation;
- process, material, nozzle or optical resolution, layer profile, and post-process;
- required repeatability, randomness, variation, and acceptance criteria.

Do not use subjective terms such as `fine`, `rough`, or `organic` without measurable parameters or a comparison coupon.

### 2. Choose the geometric family

Use [references/texture-taxonomy.md](references/texture-taxonomy.md). Select one primary representation:

- swept curves for strands, loops, braids, waves, veins, and cables;
- repeated instances for scales, studs, tiles, stitches, and discrete motifs;
- cell or graph geometry for partitioned, honeycomb, and porous networks;
- implicit or volumetric fields for smoothly blended cellular forms;
- scalar height fields for shallow relief;
- slicer perturbation for non-portable process texture.

Do not force a topological structure into a shallow grayscale bump map when crossings, holes, or connectivity matter.

### 3. Choose the mapping domain

Choose planar, cylindrical, spherical, triplanar, UV or patch-based, surface-intrinsic, or volumetric mapping from the base shape and required continuity. Place seams, poles, and transitions deliberately.

Use a mask to preserve datums, seals, bed contact, holes, interfaces, text, thin edges, and support-contact regions. Add a smooth falloff where an abrupt texture boundary would create a notch or visible step.

### 4. Parameterize intent

Expose parameters that survive a tool change:

- pattern scale, pitch, phase, orientation, and deterministic seed;
- strand, rib, beam, or wall section;
- cell count, cell-size range, and connectivity rule;
- relief amplitude, inward and outward share, baseline, and transition width;
- seam position, mapping origin, protected mask, and clipping rule;
- sampling resolution, tessellation limit, and process compensation.

Keep these parameters separate from the base part dimensions. Preserve an untextured source body and a non-destructive generator when the tool supports it.

### 5. Generate coarse to fine

Build a low-resolution preview first. Verify scale, flow, seam behavior, crossings, and protected regions before increasing samples or realizing instances.

For random or cellular patterns, save the seed. For repeated structures, derive count and phase so the closure seam does not create a partial motif. For curve structures, verify centerlines and crossings before adding profiles.

### 6. Protect manufacturability and function

Follow [references/mapping-and-printability.md](references/mapping-and-printability.md).

Run the buildability and support decision in `$design-functional-3d-prints` for every downward face, bridge, unsupported start, cavity, and slender feature introduced by the texture. Prefer masking, remapping, simplifying, reorienting, or splitting before adding support. Choose slicer or custom CAD support from access, surface, repeatability, and removal requirements rather than from the visual complexity of the pattern.

- Preserve a qualified base wall under inward relief.
- Avoid self-intersections, zero-thickness contacts, trapped volumes, unsupported downward faces, and isolated islands.
- Keep valleys cleanable and reachable when hygiene, paint, powder, or resin removal matters.
- Check whether texture adds stress concentrations, wear points, retractions, seams, or excessive perimeter time.
- Reorient, mask, simplify, or change process when the texture cannot be manufactured reliably.

### 7. Bake and inspect the manufacturing representation

Apply or bake the physical deformation before mesh export. Realize instances when the target exporter or slicer does not preserve them. Confirm the result is watertight, correctly oriented, and composed of intentional connected bodies.

Inspect the sliced or build-preparation result. A dense viewport preview does not prove that strands, relief, holes, or valleys survive the manufacturing pipeline.

### 8. Qualify with texture coupons

Create the smallest coupon that preserves mapping, curvature, orientation, base wall, and process behavior. Use a ladder or matrix over the uncertain scale and amplitude range. Include at least three repeats for promising production conditions.

Evaluate as applicable:

- feature survival, surface feel, visual contrast, and seam quality;
- minimum remaining wall and dimensional drift;
- support, resin, or powder removal;
- grip, wear, cleaning, fatigue, strength, flexibility, airflow, or light behavior;
- print time, file size, slicing time, and failure rate.

Promote only qualified parameter ranges into the final generator.

### 9. Deliver reproducible outputs

Provide:

- editable base model and texture generator;
- texture family, mapping method, mask, seed, and named parameters;
- a baked manufacturing mesh or supported implicit or lattice representation;
- explicit units, orientation, process, and material condition;
- source appearance maps when used, with license and provenance;
- coupon results, accepted range, and known limits.

Never deliver only a shader screenshot when physical texture was requested.
